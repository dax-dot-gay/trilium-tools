Sig Figs

Sig Figs

Rules:

  • If there is a decimal point, all units between the first nonzero digit on the left to the last (any) digit (inclusive) on the right is a sig fig.
  • If there isn't a decimal point, all digits between the first and last nonzero digits (inclusive) are sig figs
  • Any converting to n sig figs rounds.

Vectors

Vectors
  • Vectors have both magnitude & direction [Vectors]
    • Notation:$\overrightarrow{A}$for the full vector, and$|\overrightarrow{A}|$or$A$for just the magnitude
  • Scalars are just magnitude [Scalars]

Unit Vector Notation:

SeeUnit Vectors

Vectors can be written as along , or in their individual components

Notation for above is either$\overrightarrow{A} = (A_x, A_y)$or$\overrightarrow{A} = A_x \hat{i} + A_y \hat{j}$

The Hat values areUnit Vectors, whose only purpose is to display direction

Polar Coordinates:

$|\overrightarrow{A}|$= Length of hypotenuse/line between start & end

$|\overrightarrow{A}| = \sqrt{A_x^2+A_y^2}$

For angle$\theta$use trig:$tan(\theta) = \frac{A_y}{A_x}$

BE CAREFUL OF QUADRANTS!

Vector Arithmetic:

  • Graphical Method: Shows where you end up, by following the vectors
  • Adding with components:
    • Add components to eachother, allows mathematical usage (ie$\overrightarrow{C} = (A_x + B_x)\hat{i} + (A_y+B_y)\hat{j}$

Glossary

Unit Vectors

Unit Vectors

A term in a physics equation that displays only the direction of a force/velocity/etc. Does not have units.

Generally represented by a variable with a ^ above it, such as$\hat{r}$

Vectors

Vectors

Vectors are values that contain both a magnitude/size and a direction

i.e. velocity

Scalars

Scalars

Scalars are values that contain only a magnitude, such as time or speed.

Finite Automata

Finite Automata

A finite automaton is a theoretical machine with a fixed & finite amount of memory. Discovered over a period between 1941 & 1956

Push-Down Automata

Push-Down Automata

A theoretical machine with an unbounded stack of memory. Discovered between 1956 & 1961 by Noam Chomsky

Turing Machine

Turing Machine

A theoretical machine with unbounded, fully/randomly accessible memory. Discovered in 1936 by Alan Turing, our favorite gay boy
